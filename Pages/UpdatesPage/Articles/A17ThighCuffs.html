Can Thigh Cuffs Protect Astronauts' Vision in Space?

Astronauts in microgravity experience fluid shifts, where bodily fluids move toward the head, increasing intracranial pressure (ICP) and leading to vision issues, such as Spaceflight-Associated Neuro-Ocular Syndrome (SANS). This condition affects not only eyesight but also cognitive function and decision-making ability, both of which are crucial for space missions. To address this challenge, NASA is testing Veno-Constrictive Thigh Cuffs (VTCs)—a simple yet promising countermeasure designed to restrict fluid movement toward the upper body, potentially stabilizing ICP and reducing the risk of vision impairment. Researchers are using Ultrasound and Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) to track how different pressure levels (50-75 mmHg) affect fluid dynamics in astronauts, both in flight and during ground-based studies.

The implications of this study extend beyond space. If proven effective, VTCs could serve as a wearable solution for individuals with neurological conditions like idiopathic intracranial hypertension (IIH) or glaucoma, where elevated ICP plays a role in vision loss. Protecting vision isn’t just about eyesight—it’s about ensuring optimal cognitive performance and situational awareness in high-stakes environments, whether in deep space or on Earth. As we push forward with long-duration space travel, understanding how wearable technology can enhance human adaptability will be key. Could innovative pressure-based solutions be the next breakthrough in astronaut health?
